MIT Game Lab | Programmer

At MIT Game Lab, I worked on a special relativity simulator known as "Einstein's Playground". The project was developed to be shown accoss planetariums to help educate people on the effects of relativity. User could move around this virtual world and see real effects, including doppler effects and length contractions!
[Insert picture here]
The simulator was made within a game engine called Unity. The OpenRelativity library was used and modified to support more graphics cards. Our team made major modications to the original version of Einstein's Playground. For instance, I search and found solutions to make the simulator efficent, and increased the framerate from 30fps to 130fps on the Boston scene (using a 1060GTX). Additionally modeling and scripting was done to introduce the Bern Scene, a scene that could be used by all planetariums (Bern is where Einstein worked as a patent clerk).
I am proud of the introduction I made into the game by introducing shadows to the relativity shader. Here are before and after photos of how the game look before and after these modifcations! Night and Day!

< Notice the fake shadow on the left image? Before the shader, it was a texture..

Scripting was done in C#, while shading was done in a language called ShaderLab (a mix of HSGL and CGX?). New classes were introduced into the game for features, so a lot of object oriented programming.
Einstein's Playground was a very fun project to work on!

Skills required

  • C#
  • Unity Game Engine
  • HLSL/CG/ShaderLab
  • Blender

MIT LNS - Laboratory of Nuclear Science
During my first summer at MIT, I was considering a background in Physics and Computer Science. I would eventually realize, I did not have much time for Physics. This experience reflects on that consideration.
My task for this research project was to identify beauty and charm particles though statistical methods, mainly through the use of fitting histrograms to Gaussians and Possion distributions. All the fitting was done by using C/C++ and CERN's library of statistical tools called "ROOT".
Towards the end of the summer, I learned a bit about neural networks and attempted to used it to determine charm/beauty particles. The training was not as sucessful as I had hoped, but I learned something out of it.

Skills required

  • Cern's ROOT package
  • Python
